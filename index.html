On Beginnings

The one who started out being an ass. And stayed anyway.

Some stories begin with grand gestures. Ours began with a coincidence neither of us thought twice about.

❦

There is a peculiar beauty in the way life introduces us to the people who will eventually alter it forever. It rarely announces itself with fanfare, nor does it arrive wrapped in certainty — more often it disguises itself as an ordinary day, an ordinary conversation, a fleeting encounter that seems destined to fade into memory. For us, it began on 25 July 2025. Not with a declaration, not with destiny written across the stars, not with the kind of beginning people write novels about. Just two strangers whose paths happened to cross in a random Standoff lobby — two usernames, @santoshvadapav and @sel, one match, one conversation that had no business turning into anything. It could have ended exactly where it began, another stranger forgotten before the next round. Instead, it became us.

From the very beginning, I think a part of me was drawn to you — not in the loud, unmistakable way people describe it, but quieter, softer, revealing itself through lingering conversations and reasons to text you again. I never had the courage to say it outright, so I hid it behind teasing and words I could dismiss as harmless if they were never returned. One of those words was "cutie." I remember calling you that once, only for you to gently tell me not to — you explained you were with someone else, and that it wouldn't be right. Looking back, I admire you for that more than I probably did then. You were choosing respect when it would have been easier to ignore it.

At the time, though, all I heard was that I'd misunderstood everything, so I quietly decided to step back. No dramatic farewell, no confession, just distance — I told myself our story was only ever meant to be a brief crossing of paths. Funny how confidently we write endings for stories that have barely begun, because the universe had no intention of letting ours end there. You had, by the way, an extraordinary talent for testing my patience even before any of this — stubborn, insufferable, endlessly out to provoke me — and somehow you'd already become the person I looked forward to hearing from. Not long after I'd pulled away, you messaged me again. I replied without thinking twice. That was the last time either of us tried to end it.

People assume there was a stretch where we deliberately went quiet on each other. The truth is far less dramatic — I'd gone away for a few days, and life simply swept me into its rhythm. The silence was never a reflection of how I felt about you, only circumstance. What amazes me now is how impossible that version of us feels. Five days became three, three became one, one became every day, every day became every hour, every hour became every insignificant detail that somehow felt incomplete unless you knew about it. Today I can't imagine five hours going by without wanting to tell you something — a real thought, a terrible meme, or just because my day feels incomplete until you've been part of it.

Then came 27 May 2026 — the day friendship quietly stepped aside for something more, the day you asked me to be yours. I don't return to that memory because it was extravagant. I return to it because it was sincere, because it was ours. It was the moment I realised home isn't necessarily a place you return to — sometimes home is simply the person whose presence makes every uncertain part of life feel wonderfully certain.

Love, I have discovered, is rarely found in extraordinary moments. It exists in consistency, in patience, in choosing the same person, over and over again.

What I admire most about you isn't merely your kindness, though there's plenty of that — it's your remarkable steadiness. Where my thoughts race ahead, yours remain composed. Where I imagine storms, you somehow find calm seas. There were moments I was difficult to understand, overwhelmed by feelings I could barely explain myself, and not once did you make me feel burdensome. You listened. You reassured. You stayed.

Perhaps that's why I find the phrase "I love you" increasingly inadequate — not because it lacks sincerity, but because three words can't possibly contain everything I feel when I think of you. I can roast you into the ground and you come back with the exact same energy instead of sulking about it, and that might be the thing I trust most — that we can be ridiculous with each other and it never once tips into cruel. If there's one wish I carry into every day after this one, it's simple: may we never stop choosing each other. May the ordinary Tuesdays keep feeling like enough. May we always be the kind of story where two people just decide to stay.
